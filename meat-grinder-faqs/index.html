Meat Grinder questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

When shopping for a meat grinder, focus on motor power (higher amps for bigger jobs), build quality (stainless steel vs. aluminum), and the types of meat you plan to grind. Electric models save time and effort, but manual grinders offer control and portability. The best choice depends on how often you grind and how much volume you need to handle.

What size motor do I need for grinding meat?

Motor power is measured in amperage or horsepower, and bigger isn’t always better – it depends on what you’re grinding. For occasional home batches of boneless cuts, a motor in the mid-range (around 500 to 800 watts) will work fine. If you plan to grind tough cuts or include some tendon and sinew, step up to a more powerful model (often 1000 watts and above) so the machine doesn’t labor or overheat. Lower‑powered grinders can handle smaller amounts but may struggle with larger quantities or dense meat. A good rule of thumb: match the motor to your typical batch size and the toughness of your ingredients. A powerful motor isn’t mandatory for occasional use, but it makes the process faster and smoother.

Electric vs. manual meat grinders: which is better?

Electric grinders are the clear winner for convenience and volume. Plug in, feed the meat, and let the motor do the work. They’re ideal for frequent grinding or for processing large cuts (like whole pork shoulder or venison). Most electric models come with multiple grinding plates and a stuffing tube for sausage making, which adds versatility. Manual (hand‑crank) grinders are lighter, quieter, and don’t need an outlet – great for camping, small kitchens, or for people who grind only a few pounds at a time. They give you complete control over speed and texture, but they require arm strength and take longer. Choose manual if you value portability and price, and electric if you prioritise speed and ease.

What is the difference between a meat grinder and a food grinder?

A dedicated meat grinder is built specifically for raw meat – its feed screw, blades, and plates are designed to handle fibrous, semi‑frozen meat without clogging. The motor is engineered to manage the resistance of sinew and fat. A food grinder (often sold as a “grain mill” or “food mill”) usually has a different blade design and smaller openings, better suited for soft fruits, vegetables, or cooked items. In practice, many modern electric meat grinders can also process vegetables, bread, or cheese if you use the correct plates and clean thoroughly between uses. But using a food grinder for raw meat is not recommended – it won’t handle the texture and can overheat or break. Stick to a unit that’s explicitly sold as a meat grinder for that job.

How often should I clean my meat grinder?

Clean it immediately after every use. Raw meat leaves fat, residue, and bacteria inside the grinding head and plates. Disassemble all removable parts – the auger, blade, plates, and any pusher – and wash them in hot, soapy water, then rinse well. Dry everything thoroughly before reassembling. A deep clean after each session prevents off flavours and old meat residue from contaminating fresh batches. For electric grinders, never submerge the motor housing; wipe it with a damp cloth. Some parts are dishwasher‑safe (check the manual), but hand washing is gentler and ensures no grease gets missed.

Can I grind other foods besides meat?

Absolutely – many meat grinders double as versatile kitchen tools. With the right plates, you can grind vegetables (onions, peppers, carrots), cheese (firm varieties only), bread for homemade breadcrumbs, nuts, and even frozen fruits for sorbet or ice cream bases. Always use appropriate plates (coarse for nuts, medium for vegetables) and avoid extremely hard items like whole coffee beans or frozen bones. Just be aware that grinding very wet or sugary foods can make the machine sticky and harder to clean. It’s best to grind meat last (or dedicate separate parts) if you want to avoid cross‑contamination. Some manufacturers sell extra plate and blade sets for non‑meat use.

What should I look for in a meat grinder?

Focus on three areas: motor power, build material, and included accessories. Stainless‑steel grinding plates and blades resist rust and stay sharp longer than coated steel. A sturdy housing (die‑cast metal or high‑grade plastic) handles vibration better. Check for a built‑in circuit breaker or overload protection on electric models. Also look at the feed tray size – larger trays let you feed more meat at once. A pusher that fits snugly is a safety must. If you plan to make sausages, ensure the unit comes with stuffing tubes. And consider noise level: some budget electric grinders are surprisingly loud, while mid‑range models are usually quieter. User reviews are your best gauge for real‑world durability.

Frequently asked questions

Can I grind bones in a meat grinder?

Only if the grinder is specifically rated for bones. Most home models with motors under 1000 watts will struggle or be damaged by chicken or pork bones. Heavy‑duty electric grinders (often labelled “commercial” or “game”) can handle softer bones, but always check the manufacturer’s guidelines before trying.

Do I need to pre‑cut meat before grinding?

Yes, always cut meat into strips or cubes that fit easily into the feed tube – about 1 inch by 1 inch. This prevents jams and helps the grinder run smoothly. Partially freezing the meat (until firm but not frozen solid) also makes cutting easier and produces cleaner grinds.

How do I keep the meat cold during grinding?

Chill all parts that contact meat – the blade, plates, and even the grinder head – in the freezer for 15 to 30 minutes before starting. Also keep the meat in the fridge or freezer until the moment you grind. Grinding in short batches and returning the ground meat to the fridge between passes helps maintain a safe temperature.

What is the best meat grinder for home use?

There’s no single “best” – it depends on your volume. For occasional small batches, a mid‑range electric model from reputable brands like KitchenAid (as an attachment) works well. For regular grinding of larger amounts (10+ pounds), a dedicated electric grinder with a motor in the upper watt range offers more speed and power.

Can I grind frozen meat without thawing it?

You can grind partially frozen meat (just below 32°F), but never fully frozen blocks. Partially frozen meat cuts cleaner and stays cold, which reduces bacterial growth. Most home grinders can handle semi‑frozen meat, but if the grinder starts to strain, thaw the meat a bit more or cut it into smaller pieces.

How do I sharpen the blades on my meat grinder?

Blades eventually dull from use. For minor touch‑ups, you can use a fine diamond sharpening stone or a specialised meat grinder blade sharpener. Always follow the blade’s angle (typically around 20 degrees). If you’re not comfortable sharpening, replacement blades are widely available and affordable.

In shortBuying a meat grinder comes down to matching the machine to your kitchen habits. Think about how much meat you’ll grind, how often, and whether you’ll use it for other tasks. A well‑chosen grinder pays for itself in fresher, custom‑ground meat and opens up sausage making, burger blending, and more. Take your time, read user reviews, and you’ll be set for years of clean, satisfying grinding.
