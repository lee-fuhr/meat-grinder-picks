How to choose the right meat grinder

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

For most home cooks, a mid-range electric grinder with a motor in the 500–800 watt range, stainless steel grinding plates, and a &#35;8 or &#35;10 size offers the best balance of speed, durability, and price for occasional to weekly use. Manual grinders are quiet and budget-friendly but slow for large batches; heavy-duty models with more power suit frequent sausage making or grinding tougher meats.

How to choose between manual and electric grinders

Manual meat grinders are the quietest and most affordable option, ideal if you only grind small batches (a few pounds at a time) and don’t mind a bit of elbow grease. They’re also fully portable and have no motor to burn out. The trade-off is speed and convenience, you’ll work through a Saturday sausage project noticeably slower. Electric grinders range from compact models suitable for a few pounds per session to powerful machines that can handle dozens of pounds in one go. If you plan to grind regularly or in bulk, electric is the practical choice. Look for models that list continuous duty ratings (not just peak wattage) to gauge how long they can run without overheating.

How much motor power do you actually need?

Most home electric grinders advertise motor power between 300 and 1000 watts. For occasional grinding of boneless meats for burgers or meatballs, a lower-end model (around 300–500 watts) will work fine. If you frequently grind tough cuts, brisket, or add sinew and fat, stepping up to 500–800 watts gives you faster throughput and less risk of stalling. Above 800 watts you’re in semi-commercial territory, those machines are heavier, louder, and cost more, but they breeze through large batches and can handle smaller bones if the manufacturer explicitly states it. The key takeaway: match wattage to the volume and toughness of what you grind most often.

Should you look for stainless steel or aluminum parts?

Stainless steel grinding plates, blades, and housings are the durable choice. They resist corrosion from acidic meats (like added vinegar or citrus in marinades) and clean easily without reacting with food. Many mid-range and all high-end grinders now use stainless for the cutting components, with the main body sometimes still aluminum. Aluminum is lighter and cheaper, but it can pit or discolor over time, and some users report a metallic taste if not dried immediately after washing. If you plan to keep the grinder for years and grind often, prioritize stainless steel for any part that touches the meat. The housing material matters less, plastic or die-cast aluminum bodies are fine as long as the internal parts are stainless.

Which accessories and attachments are worth having?

A stuffing tube for sausages is the most useful extra; without it you’re limited to ground meat. Check that the tube is included or affordable separately. Different plate sizes (coarse, medium, fine) let you vary texture, coarse for chili, fine for emulsified sausages. Some grinders come with a kibbe attachment for making stuffed meat shells or a vegetable shredder cone. Avoid paying extra for gimmicky extras you won’t use. Instead, look at whether the brand offers additional plates or a foot pedal switch for hands-free operation. For most home users, a standard set of two sizes plus a stuffing tube covers everything from burgers to breakfast sausages.

What size grinder do you need for your household?

Grinders are sized by the diameter of the grinding plate, labeled as &#35;5, &#35;8, &#35;10, or &#35;12. A &#35;5 is the smallest, best for occasional grinding of a pound or two, it’s slow for bigger jobs. A &#35;8 is the sweet spot for a typical family; it handles 10–20 pounds per session without bogging down. If you process whole animals, grind 50-plus pounds at a time, or make large batches of sausage for events, a &#35;10 or &#35;12 will cut time significantly. The downside is weight and storage space: larger grinders are bulkier and more expensive. Think about your maximum batch size and work backward.

How to clean and maintain your grinder properly

Disassemble the grinder after every use and rinse all parts in cold water immediately (hot water sets fat and protein). Scrubbing with a nylon brush or a dedicated grinder cleaning brush works better than sponges. Dry every piece thoroughly to prevent rust, especially the grinder plates and blades. Some manufacturers recommend freezing the parts for a few minutes before cleaning to harden fat for easier removal. Over time, plates and blades dull. Replace them when you notice the grinder tearing rather than cutting cleanly (a sign the meat smears). For electric models, check the user manual for lubrication points, often just a drop of food-safe oil on the auger shaft. Properly cared for, a good grinder can last decades.

Frequently asked questions

Can a meat grinder grind bones?

Most home grinders are not designed for bones. Only heavy-duty models specifically rated for bone (often labeled “commercial” or “#12” and above) can handle small poultry or rib bones without damage. Attempting to grind bones in a standard home unit can dull the blades, burn out the motor, or void the warranty.

What is the difference between coarse and fine grinding plates?

Coarse plates (typically 3/8-inch or larger holes) give a chunky texture perfect for chili or burgers. Fine plates (1/8-inch or smaller) produce a smoother, almost paste-like texture used for hot dogs, bologna, or firm sausages. Many recipes call for grinding once through coarse and then again through fine for the best consistency.

Is it worth getting a grinder with a reverse function?

Yes, especially if you grind tough or sinewy meats. The reverse function clears jams that happen when long gristle wraps around the auger. It’s a small convenience that can save a lot of time and frustration during a big batch; many mid-range and higher-end grinders include it as standard.

How much meat can a typical home grinder handle in one batch?

Small grinders (like #5) are comfortable with up to about 10–15 pounds per session before the motor needs a rest. Mid-size (#8) models can manage 20–30 pounds, and large (#10 or #12) units can process 50 pounds or more continuously. Always follow the manufacturer’s duty-cycle guidance to avoid overheating.

Do I need to chill the meat before grinding?

Absolutely. Meat should be very cold, ideally around 30–32°F (just above freezing). Chilling keeps the fat from smearing, ensures cleaner cuts, and prevents the grinder from clogging. Some users pre-freeze the grinder parts (except the motor housing) for 15 minutes before starting.

Can I use a meat grinder for vegetables or making nut butters?

It depends on the model and the accessory. Many electric grinders come with a vegetable shredding or juicing cone, and some can handle soft nuts (like almonds) with a fine plate. But wet or fibrous vegetables (like tomatoes or corn) can gum up the works. Check your manual, dedicated food processors are usually better for those tasks.

In shortChoosing the right meat grinder comes down to honesty about how often you’ll use it, how much you grind at once, and what you’ll put through it. A well-made, appropriately sized grinder that’s easy to clean will pay for itself over time in fresher, better-tasting ground meat and sausage. Invest in the one that fits your routine, not the one with the biggest motor on the shelf.
